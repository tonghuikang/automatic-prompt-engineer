The basic idea is to create a network of interconnected concepts, where each concept is a node and the relationships between them are represented by the links.

The key components of a semantic network are:

1. Nodes: These represent the concepts or entities in the knowledge domain.
2. Links: These represent the relationships between the nodes, such as "is-a", "part-of", "has-property", etc.
3. Labels: These provide additional information about the nodes and links, such as the type of relationship or the specific properties of a concept.

Semantic networks can be used for a variety of applications, such as natural language processing, knowledge representation, and reasoning. They provide a flexible and intuitive way to model and organize knowledge, making it easier to understand and manipulate.

The way semantic networks work is by leveraging the relationships between concepts to infer new information or to answer questions. For example, if you know that "a dog is-a animal" and "a poodle is-a dog", then you can infer that "a poodle is-a animal" using the transitivity of the "is-a" relationship.
